Part 1: Probability Distributions

What to expect when you expect the unexpected

What is a Probability Distribution?

The Weather Forecast Analogy

A weather forecast does not just say "it will rain." It says "there is a 70% chance of rain."

A probability distribution tells you ALL the possible outcomes and how likely each one is.

A Distribution Answers:

  • What values are possible?
  • How likely is each value?
  • What is the "typical" value?
  • How spread out are the values?

Examples:

  • Grades: 0-5 scale, most around 3.5
  • Heights: Most around average, few extremes
  • Income: Few very high, many low/medium
  • Coin flip: 50% heads, 50% tails

Part 2: Confidence Intervals

Estimating with humility

Confidence Intervals: The Archery Analogy

Shooting Arrows at a Target

You want to hit the bullseye (the TRUE population parameter). But you are shooting blind, guided only by your sample data.

Point Estimate (Single Arrow)

      ___________
     /           \
    /      O      \     <-- Bullseye (true value)
   |     (X)       |    <-- Your single shot
   |               |        (probably missed!)
    \             /
     \___________/

"The average is exactly 3.8"

Precise but probably wrong

Confidence Interval (Larger Target)

      ___________
     /           \
    /   [=====O===]  \  <-- 95% CI captures bullseye!
   |    3.6    3.8    4.0
   |               |
    \             /
     \___________/

"The average is between 3.6 and 4.0"

Less precise but more likely correct

The Trade-off

Narrower interval = More precise but more likely to miss. Wider interval = Less precise but more likely to capture truth.

What Does "95% Confident" Actually Mean?

WRONG Interpretation

"There is a 95% chance the true value is in this interval"

The true value is fixed. It is either in the interval or not. There is no probability involved for a fixed value.

CORRECT Interpretation

"If we repeated this study 100 times, about 95 of those intervals would contain the true value"

The METHOD succeeds 95% of the time, not this specific interval.

100 Studies, 100 Confidence Intervals

True value: --------|-------- (fixed!)

Study 1:    [=========]              (captured)
Study 2:        [=========]          (captured)
Study 3:  [=========]                (captured)
Study 4:                [=========]  (MISSED!)   <-- 5 out of 100 will miss
Study 5:      [=========]            (captured)
...

Building a Confidence Interval

The Formula

CI = sample mean +/- (critical value) x (standard error)

      x-bar +/- t * (s / sqrt(n))

Sample Mean (x-bar)

Your best single guess

Critical Value (t)

Depends on confidence level (1.96 for 95%)

Standard Error (SE)

s / sqrt(n) - decreases with larger samples

Key Insight: The more data you collect (larger n), the smaller your standard error, and the narrower your confidence interval. More data = more precision!

Confidence Intervals in Python

from scipy import stats import numpy as np import pandas as pd # Load education data df = pd.read_csv('education_statistics.csv') # Sample data: graduation rates by school data = df['graduation_rate'].dropna() # Calculate confidence interval for the mean mean = data.mean() sem = stats.sem(data) # Standard Error of the Mean n = len(data) # 95% confidence interval using t-distribution ci_95 = stats.t.interval( confidence=0.95, df=n-1, # degrees of freedom loc=mean, # center (sample mean) scale=sem # spread (standard error) ) print(f"Sample Mean: {mean:.2f}") print(f"95% CI: ({ci_95[0]:.2f}, {ci_95[1]:.2f})")
Output Example: Sample Mean: 78.5, 95% CI: (76.2, 80.8) - We are 95% confident the population mean graduation rate is between 76.2% and 80.8%.

Part 3: Hypothesis Testing

Is this real or just random chance?

The Coin Flip Problem

Your friend claims to have a "special" coin

He flips it 10 times and gets 7 heads. Is the coin unfair, or was he just lucky?

The Skeptic (Null Hypothesis)

"The coin is fair. Getting 7 heads was just luck."

H0: P(heads) = 0.50

This is the "boring" assumption we start with.

Your Friend (Alternative Hypothesis)

"The coin is biased toward heads!"

H1: P(heads) > 0.50

This is what we want to prove.

The Question

How unlikely is 7 heads out of 10 flips IF the coin is actually fair?

The Logic of Hypothesis Testing

The Courtroom Analogy

In a trial, the defendant is "innocent until proven guilty."

We do not prove innocence. We ask: "Is there enough evidence to reject innocence?"

The 4-Step Process

1

State hypotheses

H0 vs H1

2

Collect data

Sample

3

Calculate p-value

How weird?

4

Make decision

Reject H0?

Key Insight: We NEVER "accept" the null hypothesis. We either "reject H0" (enough evidence) or "fail to reject H0" (not enough evidence). Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence!

What is a P-Value?

The p-value answers one specific question

"If the null hypothesis were true, how likely would we see data THIS extreme (or more extreme)?"

Coin Flip: P-Value Calculation

If coin is fair, P(heads) = 0.50

P(7 or more heads in 10 flips) = ?

   7 heads: 0.117
   8 heads: 0.044
   9 heads: 0.010
  10 heads: 0.001
  ----------------
  p-value:  0.172 (17.2%)

Interpretation: Getting 7+ heads happens ~17% of the time
               with a fair coin. Not that unusual!

Small p-value (< 0.05)

"This result would be RARE if H0 were true"

Evidence AGAINST the null

Large p-value (>= 0.05)

"This result is NOT unusual if H0 were true"

No evidence against the null

P-Value: Common Mistakes

Wrong Statement Why It Is Wrong Correct Statement
"p = 0.03 means 3% chance H0 is true" P-value is NOT the probability H0 is true "If H0 were true, we would see this data only 3% of the time"
"p = 0.03 means 97% chance H1 is true" P-value says nothing about H1 being true "The data is inconsistent with H0"
"p = 0.06 means no effect exists" Absence of evidence != evidence of absence "We don't have enough evidence to reject H0"
"Smaller p-value = larger effect" P-value depends on sample size, not effect size "P-value measures evidence against H0, not effect magnitude"
Critical Warning: A statistically significant result (p < 0.05) does NOT mean the effect is large, important, or practically meaningful. Always report BOTH the p-value AND the effect size.

Why 0.05? The Significance Threshold

It Is Just a Convention

Ronald Fisher, in the 1920s, suggested 0.05 as a "convenient" cutoff. It stuck. But there is nothing magical about 5%.

p < 0.05

*

"Statistically significant"

p < 0.01

**

"Highly significant"

p < 0.001

***

"Very highly significant"

Some Fields Use Stricter Thresholds

Particle physics: p < 0.0000003 (5-sigma)

Genome studies: p < 0.00000005

Some Fields Use Looser Thresholds

Exploratory research: p < 0.10

Social sciences: p < 0.05 (standard)

Two Types of Errors

H0 is True (No Effect) H0 is False (Real Effect)
Reject H0 Type I Error (False Positive) - "Crying wolf" Correct Decision (Power)
Fail to Reject H0 Correct Decision Type II Error (False Negative) - "Missing the wolf"

Type I Error (False Positive)

Alpha (a) - Usually set at 0.05

Example: Concluding a drug works when it does not

The boy who cried wolf

Type II Error (False Negative)

Beta (b) - Often around 0.20

Example: Missing a drug that actually works

Missing the real wolf

Trade-off: Reducing Type I errors (stricter threshold) increases Type II errors, and vice versa. Choose based on which error is more costly!

Part 4: Hypothesis Tests in Python

scipy.stats to the rescue

One-Sample T-Test

"Is my sample mean different from a known value?"

Example: Testing if Graduation Rate Differs from National Average

National average graduation rate: 75%. Our sample of schools shows 78%. Is this significantly different?

from scipy import stats import pandas as pd # Load education data df = pd.read_csv('education_statistics.csv') sample = df['graduation_rate'].dropna() # Test: Is sample mean different from 75? # H0: mean = 75 (no difference) # H1: mean != 75 (different) t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_1samp(sample, popmean=75) print(f"Sample mean: {sample.mean():.2f}") print(f"T-statistic: {t_stat:.3f}") print(f"P-value: {p_value:.4f}") if p_value < 0.05: print("Result: Significantly different from 75%") else: print("Result: Not significantly different from 75%")

Two-Sample T-Test

"Is there a significant difference between two groups?"

Example: Urban vs Rural Dropout Rates

Do urban schools have different dropout rates than rural schools?

from scipy import stats # Separate data by area type urban = df[df['area_type'] == 'urban']['dropout_rate'].dropna() rural = df[df['area_type'] == 'rural']['dropout_rate'].dropna() # Test: Are the means different? # H0: mean_urban = mean_rural (no difference) # H1: mean_urban != mean_rural (different) t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_ind(urban, rural) print(f"Urban mean: {urban.mean():.2f}%") print(f"Rural mean: {rural.mean():.2f}%") print(f"Difference: {urban.mean() - rural.mean():.2f} percentage points") print(f"P-value: {p_value:.4f}") if p_value < 0.05: print("Significant difference exists") else: print("No significant difference")

How to Report Statistical Results

Wrong Way

"The drug worked (p < 0.05)"

  • - No effect size
  • - No confidence interval
  • - No sample size
  • - Vague conclusion

Right Way

"Patients in the treatment group (n=150) showed a 12% improvement (95% CI: 8-16%, p = 0.003) compared to placebo"

  • + Sample size
  • + Effect size (12%)
  • + Confidence interval
  • + Exact p-value
Template for Reporting:
"[Group A] showed a [effect size] [increase/decrease] compared to [Group B] (n = [sample size], 95% CI: [lower, upper], p = [value])."
