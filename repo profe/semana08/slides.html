Tufte's Data-Ink Ratio

*

Edward Tufte: "The best visualization is one where removing any element would reduce the data communicated."

The Principle:

Data-Ink Ratio = Ink used for data / Total ink used

Goal: Maximize this ratio (get it as close to 1 as possible)

Non-Data Ink (Remove It)

  • 3D effects and shadows
  • Decorative gridlines
  • Background images
  • Excessive borders
  • Redundant labels

Data Ink (Keep It)

  • Bars, lines, points (the data itself)
  • Axis labels (minimum needed)
  • Clear title
  • Legend (only if necessary)
  • Key annotations
CONTENT

Data-Ink Ratio: Before and After

Low Data-Ink Ratio

Chart includes:

- Heavy gridlines (both directions)

- 3D effect on bars

- Gradient fills

- Drop shadows

- Box around legend

- Border around chart

- Decorative background

Ratio: ~30% data ink

High Data-Ink Ratio

Chart includes:

- Flat bars (the data)

- Minimal axis labels

- Clear title

- White background

- No gridlines (or subtle horizontal)

- Direct labels on bars

Nothing else.

Ratio: ~90% data ink

Tufte's Test: Look at your chart. Can you remove anything without losing data? If yes, remove it.

The 5-Second Rule

If someone cannot understand your chart in 5 seconds, it is too complex.

What Should Be Instant

  • What am I looking at? (title)
  • What is being compared? (x-axis)
  • What is being measured? (y-axis)
  • What is the main takeaway?

Signs of Complexity

  • More than 5-7 categories
  • Multiple y-axes
  • Legend with 10+ items
  • Need to read fine print

The Test

Show your chart to someone for 5 seconds, then hide it. Ask them: "What did you see?"

If they cannot answer, simplify.

Choosing the Right Chart

*

Chart selection is like choosing transportation: You would not take a bicycle to cross an ocean or a plane to go next door.

Question You Are Answering Chart Type Example
How do categories compare? Bar chart Cases by age group
How did something change over time? Line chart Monthly cases 2020-2024
What is the relationship between two things? Scatter plot Age vs. report delay
What is the distribution? Histogram Distribution of victim ages
What are the parts of a whole? Pie / Stacked bar Types of abuse (only if 2-5 parts!)

Bar Charts: The Workhorse

Use for comparing discrete categories. The most versatile chart type.

Common Mistakes

  • Y-axis not starting at zero (distorts comparison)
  • Too many bars (more than 10-12)
  • Unsorted bars (random order)
  • 3D effects (impossible to read values)
  • Rainbow colors (no meaning)

Best Practices

  • Start y-axis at zero
  • Sort by value (descending usually)
  • Use horizontal bars for long labels
  • One color, highlight the key bar
  • Direct label values on bars

When to use horizontal bars: When category names are long or when you have many categories (easier to read).

CONTENT

Line Charts: Showing Trends

Use for continuous data over time. The line implies a connection between points.

Common Mistakes

  • Using for non-continuous data
  • Too many lines (spaghetti chart)
  • Truncated y-axis (exaggerates trends)
  • Missing data points without indication
  • Dual y-axes (confusing correlation)

Best Practices

  • Maximum 4-5 lines
  • Highlight the important line with color
  • Label lines directly (not in legend)
  • Show data points if few observations
  • Indicate gaps or missing data

Warning: A line implies continuity. Do not use lines for categorical data (months as categories) unless you mean to show the trend.

CONTENT

Scatter Plots: Finding Relationships

Use to show the relationship between two numeric variables.

What to Look For

  • Positive relationship (up and to the right)
  • Negative relationship (down and to the right)
  • No relationship (random scatter)
  • Clusters (groups in the data)
  • Outliers (points far from the rest)

Best Practices

  • Add a trend line if appropriate
  • Use transparency for overlapping points
  • Color/size can encode a third variable
  • Label outliers or interesting points
  • Keep axes proportional to data range

Remember: Correlation does not imply causation. Ice cream sales and drownings both increase in summer, but ice cream does not cause drowning!

Pie Charts: Use With Extreme Caution

*

Pie charts are the most misused chart type. Humans are bad at comparing angles and areas.

When NOT to Use

  • More than 5 categories
  • Similar-sized slices
  • Need to compare exact values
  • Data does not sum to 100%
  • Comparing multiple pies

In most cases, a bar chart is better!

When Pie Charts Work

  • 2-3 categories only
  • One category dominates (showing majority)
  • Parts of a whole message
  • Audience expects pie (budgets, shares)

"75% of cases are in category X"

Alternative: Consider a stacked bar chart or waffle chart for part-to-whole relationships with more categories.

Gray is your best friend. Color is the exception, not the rule.

Color Meanings and Accessibility

Cultural Associations

 Blue Trust, calm, professional
Green Good, growth, go
Red Danger, error, stop
Orange/Yellow Warning, attention
Gray Neutral, secondary

Colorblind Accessibility

8% of men have some form of color blindness.

  • Never rely on color ALONE
  • Avoid red-green as only differentiator
  • Use patterns, labels, or shapes too
  • Test with colorblind simulators

Safe palette: Blue + Orange is colorblind-friendly. Blue + Gray for emphasis is always safe.

The Visualization Checklist

Before sharing any chart, verify:

Content

  • Title tells the story (not just describes)
  • Axes are labeled clearly
  • Data source is cited
  • Units are specified
  • Right chart type for the question

Design

  • Passes the 5-second rule
  • High data-ink ratio
  • Color used meaningfully
  • No 3D effects or chartjunk
  • Accessible for colorblind

Good Title vs. Bad Title

Bad: "Cases by Year"

(Describes the chart)

Good: "Cases doubled from 2020 to 2023"

(Tells the story)
